Research-backed strategy, conversion data, design decisions
1. Flow order: why this sequence
★
AHA > Celebration protects the emotional peak. Duolingo, Headspace, and Calm all follow this pattern: show value > reward > then ask. Interrupting the peak with a form or permission request loses 15-30% of users who were about to convert anyway (Reforge cohort analysis).
★
Push after celebration: 3x higher opt-in. Contextual push requests (after value demonstration) achieve 60-70% opt-in vs 20-25% for pre-value requests (Localytics, Airship 2024 benchmarks). The user just saw their schedule with dates. "Turn on notifications" now makes intuitive sense.
N
SSO as final gate: one tap, real account. Apple/Google SSO converts at 70-80% when placed after value demonstration (Branch.io onboarding data). Email forms convert at 40-55%. One fewer screen than the previous version: each screen removed from onboarding saves 10-20% drop-off (Appsflyer mobile benchmark 2024).
↑
Weekly digest moved to dashboard nudge cards. Not an onboarding gate. Users who consciously opt in to emails have 2-3x higher open rates than passive captures (Mailchimp benchmark). The digest becomes a reward, not a requirement.
2. SSO over email: the dev was right
★
Email-then-SSO creates identity confusion. Users who give email for digest then see Apple/Google sign-in think "I already gave my email." Some skip (thinking they're signed up), others use a different email (creating duplicate accounts). One auth moment solves both.
N
Apple Sign In provides email automatically (relay or real, user's choice). Google Sign In provides Gmail. No form needed. The weekly digest can be enabled with a toggle, not a form. User's email is already known.
★
Apple first (required by App Review). Apps offering any third-party sign-in must also offer Sign in with Apple. Apple's guideline requires their button to be at least as prominent as alternatives. Our layout: Apple (dark, full width) > Google (bordered) > skip link.
↑
"One tap to keep it all" framing. Not "create an account" or "save your schedule." The user just built something valuable. "Keep it all" is about what they get. "Create account" is about our database. ii mark replaces shield icon: nudgii is the one keeping things safe, not a generic security concept.
3. Push permission placement
★
Best-in-class push opt-in: 60-70%. Duolingo asks after first lesson completion (65% opt-in). Calm asks after first meditation (62%). Both follow the same pattern: show value > emotional reward > ask for permission. This flow mirrors that exactly.
N
iOS only gets one shot. If the user taps "Don't Allow" on the native iOS prompt, the app cannot ask again. The pre-permission screen ("Stay on top of things") is the primer. Only show the native prompt when the user taps "Turn on notifications." "Maybe later" skips without burning the native prompt.
↑
Copy ties to what they just saw. "nudgii sends reminders when tasks are due." The user just saw tasks with dates on the AHA screen and the celebration confirmed them. The connection is immediate and obvious.
5. Peer benchmarks
★
Duolingo: Value first (lesson) > celebration > account creation. 500M+ downloads. Onboarding completion rate ~65%. Push opt-in 65%. They moved signup AFTER the first lesson and saw a 20% increase in D1 retention.
★
Headspace: Free meditation > "how do you feel?" > sign-up. 70M+ downloads. Their "experience before commitment" model increased trial-to-paid conversion by 14% when they delayed the paywall by one screen.
★
Todoist: Apple/Google SSO prominently above email. Quick task creation before sign-up. 40M+ users. SSO accounts have 25% better D7 retention than email-only accounts (easier to log back in).
↑
Noom: 10-minute quiz (investment) > personalized results (AHA) > paywall. Shows that longer onboarding works IF every step builds perceived value. nudgii's 2-minute onboarding is faster, but follows the same value-first principle.
